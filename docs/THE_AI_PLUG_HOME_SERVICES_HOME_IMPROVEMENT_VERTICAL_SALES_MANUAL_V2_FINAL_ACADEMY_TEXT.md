# Part 1 — Home Services Foundation Academy

## Chapter 1 — What Home Services Businesses Actually Sell

Home service businesses sell trust, response, qualification, scheduling, estimates, project clarity, and follow-through. The customer has a problem or project. The business wins when it captures the right details, responds clearly, routes the request, follows up, and makes the next step easy.

Common revenue events:
- service calls
- quote requests
- estimates
- inspections
- consultations
- emergency calls
- project-based jobs
- repeat service
- maintenance plans
- referrals
- reviews
- before-and-after proof
- jobsite trust
- service-area visibility

Training outcome: The rep understands that these owners do not buy AI. They buy fewer missed jobs, cleaner intake, easier quoting, better follow-up, and organized opportunities.

## Chapter 2 — The Real Problem: Jobs Slip Through

The basic problem is opportunity leakage. A homeowner asks for help but the process behind the business is not connected.

Common leaks:
- missed calls while owner/team is on a job
- texts and photos getting buried
- website forms that do not ask enough
- quote requests with missing details
- no fast follow-up
- bad handoff between office, owner, estimator, and crew
- leads from too many channels
- poor tracking of who was contacted
- weak review/referral process after the job
- unclear pipeline visibility

Field explanation: “We are not trying to change how you do the work. We are trying to clean up the path between the homeowner asking for help and your team knowing what to do next.”

## Expanded Training Philosophy

A trainee should not memorize scripts without understanding the business situation. The rep must learn the pattern:

1. Identify the trade.
2. Identify how that business wins jobs.
3. Find where requests, details, scheduling, estimates, or follow-up leak.
4. Translate The AI Plug into that owner’s world.
5. Recommend the smallest useful next step.
6. Avoid guarantees.
7. Create clean delivery handoff notes.

The rep should sound like a practical business operator, not a software salesperson. The owner should feel that the rep understands the day-to-day chaos of calls, estimates, jobsite work, crews, customer questions, photos, and follow-up.

Day 7: practical value note or question.
Day 14: soft close-the-loop.
Day 30: check back with a new angle.

After discovery:
Same day: recap the leak and the smallest useful next step.
Two days later: ask if the recap matches what they see.
Five to seven days later: offer to review the first system.
Fourteen days later: close the loop or schedule later.

After launch:

Week 2: ask what feels unclear.
Month 1: recap requests, common questions, follow-up items, and next improvement.
